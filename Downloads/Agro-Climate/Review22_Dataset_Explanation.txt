This document explains the datasets used in the `Review22.ipynb` notebook, why they were combined, and why the resulting dataset is suitable for recurrent neural networks (RNNs/LSTMs).

## 1. The Raw Datasets

The project utilizes several distinct types of agricultural and climate data, aggregated at the US county level:

*   **yielddata.csv (Historical Crop Yield):** Contains the target variable, which is the historical corn yield (bushels per acre) for a given county and year.
*   **gddAprOct.csv (Growing Degree Days):** A measure of heat accumulation used to predict plant development rates. This file contains a massive amount of temporal data (123 columns):
    *   `gdd_gddm1` ... `gdd_gddm60`: The past 60 months of historical GDD values leading up to the harvest year. This is the crucial *time-series* component.
    *   `gdd_gdd0`: Base-year (current year) GDD.
    *   `gdd_gddp1` ... `gdd_gddpX`: Future projected GDD values.
*   **pptAprOct.csv (Precipitation):** Total precipitation for the county/year. A static climatic feature affecting crop growth.
*   **soilXXXX.csv (Soil Composition):** Examples include `soil1992.csv`, `soil2001.csv`, etc. These files define the physical properties of the soil in each county. Soil composition changes very slowly, so it is treated as a *static geospatial feature*.
*   **gridInfo.csv (Geospatial Grid Data):** Contains the geographic coordinates (latitude, longitude) translating counties into spatial locations for use in computer vision models like CNNs.

## 2. Why Combine the Columns? (The Merging Process)

The raw files exist separately because they often come from different government or research databases (e.g., USDA for yield, NOAA for climate, SSURGO for soils). To train a single machine learning model that predicts yield based on *all* contributing factors, the data must be merged.

### The Merging Logic (`load_and_merge` function):
1.  **Inner Join on `county_id` and `year` (Yield ⋈ GDD ⋈ PPT):**
    The model needs specific climate data for the exact year the crop was grown. A county must exist in all three datasets for a specific year to provide a valid training sample. If yield data exists for 1995 but weather data is missing, we cannot train on it. Thus, an *inner join* ensures only complete historical records are kept.
2.  **Left Join on `county_id` (Result + Soil + GridInfo):**
    Soil properties and geographic coordinates (lat/lon) are considered static (they barely change year-over-year). Therefore, the notebook performs a *left join* using only the `county_id`. This broadcasts the single row of soil/geo data for "County X" to *every year* of historical yield records for "County X".

**Resulting Master DataFrame (70,721 rows):**
Every single row now represents a complete feature vector for one county in one specific year:
`[60 months of past heat (sequence)] + [current heat] + [precipitation] + [soil type] + [lat/lon] -> Target: Yield (bu/acre)`

## 3. Why is this suitable for RNN / LSTM Models?

Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and Long Short-Term Memory networks (LSTMs) are specifically designed to analyze *sequential* or *time-series* data.

### The Temporal Sequence Feature
The core reason this dataset works for an LSTM is the presence of the `gdd_gddm1` to `gdd_gddm60` columns. When building the arrays for training, the `TemporalPreprocessor` class deliberately reshapes these 60 columns:

1.  **Shape Requirement:** LSTMs in PyTorch require tracking data over "time steps". The preprocessor converts the flat rows into a 3D tensor: `(Samples, TimeSteps, Features)`.
2.  **The Reshape:** A shape of `(70721, 60, 1)` means there are 70,721 individual harvest records. Each record is a sequence of **60 distinct time steps** (the 5 years of preceding months). At each time step, there is **1 feature** (the GDD value for that exact corresponding month).

### Why use an LSTM for Crop Yield?
Crop yields are non-linear and exhibit "memory." A severe drought three years ago might permanently degrade soil moisture reserves, affecting a crop grown today. Standard feed-forward networks (MLPs) struggle to understand the chronological order of these events.

*   **Sequence Memory:** Instead of treating the 60 past months as 60 random features, the LSTM processes them chronologically ($Month_{-60} \rightarrow Month_{-59} \dots \rightarrow Month_{0}$). It maintains an internal "hidden state" that accumulates the story of the climate over the past 5 years.
*   **The Architecture in `Review22.ipynb`:**
    *   The 60-month chronological sequence goes through the **LSTM**.
    *   The LSTM condenses the 5-year climatic history into a single representative vector (aided by Bahdanau Attention).
    *   Simultaneously, the *static features* (base heat, total precipitation, soil, location) and a unique embedding representing the specific county are passed through standard dense layers.
    *   Finally, the condensed 5-year history and the static features are concatenated (fused) together to make the final yield prediction.

Highest R² Score: 0.5704. R² measures how much of the variation in the crop yield can be explained by the model's inputs. A score of 0.5704 (57%) is vastly superior to the LSTM (only ~14%) and the Transformer (which completely failed to find a correlation at ~0%).
Lowest Error (RMSE): 25.6434. Root Mean Squared Error measures the average difference between the predicted yield and the actual yield, punishing large errors more heavily. The CNN had the lowest error spread by a wide margin (LSTMs and Transformers had errors near 36–39).
Lowest Absolute Error (MAE): 19.6894. On average, the CNN was off by about 19.68 bushels per acre when guessing the yield, which is much tighter than the LSTM (off by ~31) and Transformer (off by ~33).
